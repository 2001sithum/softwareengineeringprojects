Section 1: Fundamentals of Costing & Pricing

  1. 1. What three key estimates are required in project planning?

    Effort, Time, and Cost.

  2. 2. What is the dominant cost component in most software projects?

    Effort costs (primarily the salaries of engineers and associated overheads).

  3. 3. List two direct effort costs.

    1. Salaries of engineers involved in the project. 2. Social and insurance costs for the engineers.

  4. 4. List three overhead costs that must be taken into account for effort costs.

    1. Costs of building, heating, lighting. 2. Costs of networking and communications. 3. Costs of shared facilities (e.g., library, staff restaurant). (Also: social security, support staff).

  5. 5. What is the general rule of thumb for calculating total project costs from salaries?

    Total costs â‰ˆ 2 * software engineers' salaries.

  6. 6. What is the difference between 'costing' and 'pricing'?

    Costing is discovering the cost to the *developer* to produce the system. Pricing is determining the price charged to the *customer*, which is influenced by many business factors.

  7. 7. Why is the software price not a simple formula of `cost + profit`?

    Because broader organisational, economic, political, and business considerations influence the final price charged.

  8. 8. Explain how 'cost estimate uncertainty' can influence the price.

    If an organization is unsure of its cost estimate, it may increase its price by adding a contingency to cover the risk of underestimation.

  9. 9. How can 'contractual terms' affect the price of software?

    If a customer allows the developer to retain ownership of the source code for reuse, the developer may charge a lower price than if the source code must be handed over.

  10. 10. How does 'requirements volatility' influence pricing strategy?

    An organization may quote a lower price to win a contract if requirements are likely to change, knowing they can charge higher prices for the inevitable change requests later.

  11. 11. Why might a developer's 'financial health' affect the price?

    A developer in financial difficulty might lower their price significantly just to win a contract and maintain cash flow, even if it means making a smaller profit or breaking even.

  12. 12. Explain the pricing strategy related to 'market opportunity'.

    A developer may quote a low price to enter a new market segment. Accepting a low profit initially can lead to more profitable opportunities later and provide valuable experience.

  13. 13. What are the primary inputs for cost estimation?

    The project plan, which outlines the activities and scope of the project.

Section 2: Software Productivity

  1. 14. What is 'software productivity'?

    A measure of the rate at which individual engineers involved in software development produce software and associated documentation.

  2. 15. What is the essential goal of measuring productivity?

    To measure the amount of useful functionality produced per unit of time (e.g., per person-month).

  3. 16. Is productivity purely a measure of output, or is quality a factor?

    It is not a quality-oriented measure, but quality assurance is a factor in the assessment. Producing a large amount of low-quality, buggy software is not considered productive.

  4. 17. List six factors that affect individual productivity.

    Domain experience, Process quality, Project size, Technology support, Working environment, and Individual capability.

  5. 18. How does 'domain experience' affect productivity?

    An engineer with deep experience in the application domain (e.g., banking, aviation) can work much faster because they understand the requirements and context without extensive explanation.

  6. 19. How does 'process quality' affect productivity?

    A well-defined, efficient development process with good tools can significantly improve productivity by reducing wasted effort and rework.

  7. 20. What is the relationship between 'project size' and individual productivity?

    As team size increases for a larger project, individual productivity tends to decrease due to increased communication and coordination overhead.

  8. 21. How does 'technology support' affect productivity?

    Productivity is higher when engineers are supported by powerful CASE tools, fast computers, and good software development environments.

  9. 22. How does the 'working environment' impact productivity?

    A quiet, comfortable environment with adequate facilities and minimal interruptions allows engineers to concentrate and be more productive.

  10. 23. What is meant by 'individual capability'?

    The innate talent, problem-solving skills, and general experience of each individual engineer, which can vary significantly.

Section 3: Productivity Measures (LOC & Function Points)

  1. 24. Name the two main types of productivity measures.

    1. Size-related measures (e.g., Lines of Code - LOC). 2. Function-related measures (e.g., Function Points - FP).

  2. 25. What is a size-related measure based on?

    It is based on some tangible output from the software process, such as lines of delivered source code or object code instructions.

  3. 26. What is a function-related measure based on?

    It is based on an estimate of the functionality of the delivered software, independent of the programming language.

  4. 27. List three problems associated with productivity measurement in general.

    1. Estimating the size of the measure (e.g., how many FPs). 2. Estimating the total number of programmer-months that have elapsed. 3. Estimating the productivity of non-programming staff (e.g., documentation team).

  5. 28. Why is defining a 'line of code' a problem for modern languages?

    Because a single logical statement in a language like Java can span multiple physical lines, or multiple statements can be placed on a single line, making a simple line count ambiguous.

  6. 29. Explain the productivity comparison paradox with Lines of Code.

    Using LOC suggests that programmers using low-level languages (which require more lines for the same function) are more productive. It also suggests that verbose programmers are more productive than those who write compact code. Both conclusions are false.

  7. 30. What are the five program characteristics used to calculate Function Points?

    1. External inputs and outputs. 2. User interactions. 3. External interfaces. 4. Files used by the system.

  8. 31. How is the Unadjusted Function Point Count (UFC) calculated?

    `UFC = âˆ‘ (number of elements of a given type) Ã— (weight)`. Each of the five characteristics is counted, multiplied by a standard weight, and the results are summed.

  9. 32. How is the final Function Point count determined from the UFC?

    The UFC is modified by a 'complexity of the project' factor, which adjusts the count based on various system characteristics.

  10. 33. Why is automatic function-point counting considered impossible?

    Because it requires an intelligent understanding of the system's functionality to identify and classify the different elements (inputs, outputs, interactions, etc.). This is a subjective process that depends on the estimator.

  11. 34. What does LOC stand for and what does FP stand for?

    LOC: Lines of Code. FP: Function Points.

  12. 35. What is the relationship between LOC and FP?

    FPs can be used to estimate LOC. `LOC = AVC * number of function points`, where AVC is an Average number of lines of code per function point for a given language.

  13. 36. Why does the AVC (Average LOC per FP) vary between languages?

    Because different languages have different levels of expressive power. A high-level language (like a 4GL) requires very few lines of code to implement a function point, while a low-level language (like assembly) requires many more.

Section 4: Estimation Techniques

  1. 37. Why is it difficult to make an accurate initial estimate for a software project?

    Because initial estimates are often based on inadequate or incomplete information from a user requirements definition, the technology may be new, and the team members may be unknown.

  2. 38. What does it mean for project cost estimates to be 'self-fulfilling'?

    The estimate defines the budget, and the product's scope and features are then adjusted (often reduced) to fit within that budget, making the original estimate appear correct.

  3. 39. How do changing technologies impact estimation experience?

    Previous estimating experience from older technologies (e.g., mainframes) may not carry over to new technologies (e.g., distributed object systems, web services), making historical data less relevant.

  4. 40. List the five main estimation techniques discussed in the lecture.

    1. Algorithmic cost modelling. 2. Expert judgement. 3. Estimation by analogy. 4. Parkinson's Law. 5. Pricing to win.

  5. 41. Describe the 'Expert judgement' technique.

    Several experts in the application domain and technology are consulted. They each estimate the project cost, and these estimates are compared and discussed until a consensus is reached.

  6. 42. What is the main advantage and disadvantage of 'Expert judgement'?

    Advantage: Relatively cheap and can be accurate if true experts are available. Disadvantage: Very inaccurate if there are no experts with direct experience of similar systems.

  7. 43. Describe the 'Estimation by analogy' technique.

    The cost of a new project is computed by comparing it to a similar, completed project in the same application domain.

  8. 44. What is the main advantage and disadvantage of 'Estimation by analogy'?

    Advantage: Can be very accurate if good project data is available. Disadvantage: Impossible if no comparable project has been tackled before, and requires a systematically maintained cost database.

  9. 45. Describe 'Parkinson's Law' as an estimation technique.

    The project costs whatever resources are available. The cost is determined by available resources, not objective assessment. For example, if 5 people are available for 12 months, the effort is estimated as 60 person-months.

  10. 46. What is the main advantage and disadvantage of 'Parkinson's Law'?

    Advantage: No overspending. Disadvantage: The system is usually unfinished because the actual work required was not objectively assessed.

  11. 47. Describe the 'Pricing to win' technique.

    The software cost is estimated to be whatever the customer has available to spend. The effort is based on the customer's budget, not the software's functionality.

  12. 48. What is the main advantage and disadvantage of 'Pricing to win'?

    Advantage: You get the contract. Disadvantage: The probability that the customer gets the system they actually want is small.

  13. 49. Explain 'top-down' estimation.

    Start at the system level and assess the overall system functionality and how it is delivered through sub-systems.

  14. 50. Explain 'bottom-up' estimation.

    Start at the component level and estimate the effort required for each component. Then add these efforts to reach a final estimate.

  15. 51. What is a major advantage of top-down estimation?

    It can be done early without detailed knowledge of the system architecture, and it naturally takes into account system-level costs like integration and documentation.

  16. 52. What is a major disadvantage of top-down estimation?

    It can underestimate the cost of solving difficult low-level technical problems that are not yet visible.

  17. 53. What is a major advantage of bottom-up estimation?

    It can be an accurate method if the system has been designed in detail and all components are identified.

  18. 54. What is a major disadvantage of bottom-up estimation?

    It may underestimate the costs of system-level activities such as integration, configuration management, and documentation.

  19. 55. What is the recommended approach if different estimation methods produce very different results?

    This indicates that you have insufficient information available to make a reliable estimate. Some action should be taken to find out more (e.g., through prototyping or further analysis) to make more accurate estimates.

Section 5: Algorithmic Cost Modelling

  1. 56. How is cost estimated in algorithmic cost modelling?

    Cost is estimated as a mathematical function of product, project, and process attributes.

  2. 57. How is the mathematical function in an algorithmic model derived?

    It is derived from a study of historical costing data from past projects.

  3. 58. What is the most commonly used product attribute for cost estimation in these models?

    Lines of code (LOC), or code size.

  4. 59. What is the general form of the effort estimation formula?

    Effort = A Ã— SizeB Ã— M

  5. 60. In the formula `Effort = A Ã— Size^B Ã— M`, what does 'A' represent?

    'A' is a constant factor derived from local, historical project data, representing the baseline productivity of the organization.

  6. 61. What does 'Size' represent?

    'Size' is an estimate of the product size, typically in thousands of lines of code (KLOC).

  7. 62. What does the exponent 'B' represent?

    'B' is an exponent that reflects the disproportionate effort required for larger projects (diseconomy of scale). It is usually between 1 and 1.5.

  8. 63. What does 'M' represent?

    'M' is a multiplier that combines various cost drivers or attributes related to the product, project, and process.

  9. 64. Name a well-known algorithmic cost model.

    The COCOMO (Constructive Cost Model).

  10. 65. What are 'cost drivers' in a model like COCOMO?

    They are attributes that affect the effort required to complete the project. Examples include product complexity, required reliability, and developer experience. Each has a rating that contributes to the multiplier 'M'.

  11. 66. What is the primary advantage of algorithmic models?

    They provide a repeatable and systematic way to generate an estimate, which can be analyzed and justified.

  12. 67. What is the primary disadvantage of algorithmic models?

    Their accuracy is highly dependent on the quality of the historical data used to calibrate the model (the constant 'A') and the accuracy of the initial size estimate.

  13. 68. Why is it difficult to estimate the size (e.g., LOC) of a system early in a project?

    Because the requirements are not yet fully understood or specified in detail, so any estimate of the final code size is a rough guess.

  14. 69. How would a high 'required reliability' cost driver affect the final effort estimate?

    It would increase the value of the multiplier 'M', which in turn would increase the final effort estimate, reflecting the extra work needed for more rigorous testing and validation.

  15. 70. How would a highly experienced team affect the effort estimate in a COCOMO-like model?

    It would decrease the value of the multiplier 'M', which would decrease the final effort estimate, reflecting the higher productivity of an experienced team.

  16. 71. Do most algorithmic models produce significantly different results?

    No, the presentation states that most models are basically similar but with different attribute values (cost drivers).

  17. 72. Is it a good practice to rely on a single estimation technique?

    No. Each method has strengths and weaknesses. Estimation should be based on several methods, and the results compared.

  18. 73. What is the relationship between project activities and cost estimation?

    Project planning involves splitting the project into activities, and cost estimation is the process of estimating the effort, time, and cost required for those activities.

  19. 74. If a project's size is doubled, does the effort also double? Explain using the algorithmic model formula.

    No, the effort more than doubles. This is because the exponent 'B' is typically greater than 1 (e.g., 1.2). If size doubles, the effort increases by a factor of 21.2, which is greater than 2. This reflects the diseconomy of scale in software projects.

  20. 75. What is the overall message of the "Software Cost Estimation" presentation?

    That software cost estimation is an inherently difficult but critical project planning activity. There is no single perfect technique, so a combination of approaches should be used to arrive at a reasonable estimate, which must be continuously refined as the project progresses.

  21. 76. Why is it important to estimate contractor productivity separately?

    Because different teams (e.g., core developers vs. a documentation team) have different skills and work rates. Lumping them together under a single productivity measure would be inaccurate.

  22. 77. What system development time data suggests that high-level languages are more productive?

    The example table shows that a high-level language required less code (1500 lines) and less total effort (20 weeks) to deliver the same system compared to assembly code (5000 lines, 28 weeks).

  23. 78. What is the main problem with Function Points being subjective?

    It makes the estimate non-repeatable. Two different estimators looking at the same requirements could arrive at very different function point counts, leading to different cost estimates.

  24. 79. When is 'pricing to win' the only applicable method?

    In highly competitive situations where the customer's budget is fixed and the primary goal is to secure the contract, even if it means delivering a reduced-scope product.

  25. 80. What is the main strength of bottom-up estimation?

    It forces a detailed analysis of the system, which can reveal technical complexities and lead to a more accurate estimate for the development work itself.

  26. 81. What costs does top-down estimation naturally account for that bottom-up might miss?

    System-level costs such as integration, configuration management, and documentation.

  27. 82. What is a 'person-month'?

    A unit of effort representing the amount of work one person can do in one month. It is a standard unit for effort estimation.

  28. 83. How could an organization improve its algorithmic cost model over time?

    By systematically collecting data from its completed projects and using that data to refine and calibrate the model's constants (like 'A') and cost drivers ('M') to better reflect its own specific context.

  29. 84. Can you use 'estimation by analogy' if your company has never built a similar system before?

    No, this technique is impossible if there is no comparable completed project to draw an analogy from.

  30. 85. What is the risk of using 'expert judgment' when the experts are not familiar with the new technology being used?

    Their experience with older technologies may not be relevant, leading them to make very inaccurate predictions about the effort required for the new system.

  31. 86. What does "self-fulfilling" imply about the project outcome?

    It implies that the final product will likely have reduced scope or functionality compared to the original vision, as it was adjusted to fit the initial budget estimate.

  32. 87. In the system development times table, why is the total effort not just the sum of the weeks for each activity?

    The table likely assumes some activities can happen in parallel or that a team is working, so the total elapsed time (effort in weeks) is based on the project schedule, not a simple sum.

  33. 88. Why must overheads like building and support staff be included in effort costs?

    Because these are real costs incurred by the organization to support the development team. The project must cover its share of these costs to be truly profitable.

  34. 89. What is the main difference in focus between 'cost estimation' and 'project planning'?

    Project planning is the broader activity of defining the work (activities). Cost estimation is a specific part of planning that focuses on predicting the resources needed for that work.

  35. 90. What is the problem with counting "object code instructions" as a productivity measure?

    It is highly dependent on the compiler and its optimization settings. Different compilers or settings can produce a different number of instructions for the exact same source code.

  36. 91. A project is estimated using expert judgment and an algorithmic model, and the results differ by 50%. What does this indicate?

    It indicates that there is insufficient information to make a reliable estimate. The assumptions behind each estimate should be re-examined, and more information should be gathered.

  37. 92. What is the 'Delphi' technique in expert judgment?

    A more formal version of expert judgment where experts provide anonymous estimates. A facilitator shares the anonymous results, and the experts revise their estimates based on the group's input, iterating until a consensus is reached.

  38. 93. If a project has high 'requirements volatility', which pricing factor becomes very important?

    The contractual terms for handling change requests. The price of changes after the contract is signed will have a major impact on the final project cost to the customer.

  39. 94. How could reuse of off-the-shelf software affect a cost estimate?

    It would reduce the development effort estimate but might add licensing costs (a hardware/software cost component) and integration effort.

  40. 95. Why is estimating documentation team productivity difficult?

    Because their output (e.g., pages of a manual) does not correlate well with the size or complexity of the software, and their work depends heavily on input from developers.

  41. 96. What is the fundamental requirement for 'estimation by analogy' to work well?

    A systematically maintained, accurate database of past project data (costs, size, effort, etc.).

  42. 97. What is the risk of a manager using Parkinson's Law to set the budget?

    The team will use all the allocated time and budget, but there is a high probability that the system will be unfinished or of low quality because the resources were not based on the actual work required.

  43. 98. How can a project manager gain more accurate information to improve an estimate?

    By investing in further requirements analysis, creating a prototype to clarify unknowns, or breaking down the system into more detailed components for a better bottom-up estimate.

  44. 99. What is the role of 'historical data' in algorithmic cost modelling?

    It is essential. Historical data from an organization's past projects is used to calibrate the model's constants and cost drivers, making the model specific and more accurate for that organization.

  45. 100. If a manager has to provide an estimate with very little information, which technique is likely to be the least accurate but sometimes necessary?

    Expert judgment. While potentially inaccurate without good data, it relies on the experience of experts to make a reasonable guess, which may be the only option in the very early stages of a project.
