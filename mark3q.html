Section 1: Fundamental Definitions (Questions 1-15)

  1. What is the first definition of "Marketing" provided in the presentation?

    Marketing is "The art and science of creating, communicating, and delivering value to satisfy the needs of a target market."

  2. What two words are highlighted in the first definition of marketing, and why are they significant?

    The highlighted words are "delivering" and "value." They are significant because they emphasize that marketing is not just about communication; it's about the entire process of providing tangible and intangible worth to the customer through the supply chain.

  3. Define "Marketing Management."

    Marketing Management is the "Coordination of strategic planning, implementation, and control of activities designed to bring about desired exchanges with target markets."

  4. How does Marketing Management relate to the broader concept of Marketing?

    Marketing Management is the practical application and execution of the broader marketing philosophy. While marketing is the overall concept of value exchange, marketing management deals with the specific planning, implementation, and control of the strategies to achieve it.

  5. State Philip Kotler's definition of marketing.

    Philip Kotler's definition is: "Marketing is the process by which companies create value for customers and build strong customer relationships in order to capture value in return."

  6. In Kotler's definition, what does "create value for customers" mean?

    It means developing products, services, and experiences that solve a customer's problem or satisfy their needs and wants effectively. This value can be functional, emotional, or social.

  7. In Kotler's definition, what does "capture value in return" refer to?

    It refers to the benefits the company receives from the customer, which include revenue, profit, customer loyalty, positive word-of-mouth, and customer lifetime value.

  8. What is the central theme of the "Iceberg Concept" in marketing?

    The central theme is that the most visible parts of marketing (Selling, Advertising) are only a small fraction of the entire process. The majority of marketing work (research, strategy, product development) is invisible to the casual observer, like the submerged part of an iceberg.

  9. According to the Iceberg Concept, which function is at the "tip"?

    Selling.

  10. What functions are considered part of the larger, submerged "Marketing" section of the iceberg?

    Market research, pricing, product design, distribution, customer relationship management, and strategic planning.

  11. Explain the relationship between Advertising and Marketing using the Venn diagram as a metaphor.

    The Venn diagram shows advertising as a smaller circle completely inside the larger circle of marketing. This means advertising is a subset of marketing; it is one of many tools within the broader marketing strategy.

  12. What is the specific focus of advertising?

    Advertising is focused specifically on "communicating a promotional message to a target audience."

  13. List three examples of "Online ads."

    Social media ads, Google search ads, and banner ads.

  14. List three examples of "Outdoor ads."

    Billboards, ads on public transit (buses, trains), and ads in bus shelters.

  15. What is the role of an influencer or celebrity endorsement in advertising?

    They are used to leverage the trust, credibility, and appeal of a well-known person to promote a product and influence the purchasing decisions of their followers.

Section 2: Core Marketing Concepts Cycle (Questions 16-40)

  1. What are the five components of the Core Marketing Concepts cycle?

    1. Needs, wants and demands. 2. Products and Services. 3. Value, satisfaction, and quality. 4. Exchange, transactions, and relationships. 5. Markets.

  2. Define a "Need" and provide three examples of basic needs.

    A need represents a state of felt deprivation of some basic satisfaction. Examples: Food, water, shelter, air, clothing.

  3. Beyond basic survival, what other types of needs do humans have?

    Humans also have needs for education, sports, and recreation, which contribute to personal development and enjoyment.

  4. Define a "Want."

    A want is a desire for specific satisfiers of deeper needs, shaped by one's culture and individual personality.

  5. How does the example of "rice, bread, and roti" illustrate the concept of a want?

    The underlying need is for food. The specific choice of rice, bread, or roti is the want, which is a culturally or personally preferred way to satisfy that need.

  6. Define "Demand."

    Demand is a want for a specific product that is backed by an ability and willingness to buy it.

  7. What two factors must be present for a want to become a demand?

    The desire for the product and the ability to purchase it (purchasing power).

  8. According to the comparison table, what is the key difference in "Fulfillment Priority" between needs and wants?

    Needs have a high fulfillment priority as they are necessary for life and health. Wants have a low fulfillment priority as they are optional and based on personal choice.

  9. Explain the difference in "Flexibility" between needs and wants.

    Needs are non-negotiable and fundamental. Wants are flexible and subject to personal preferences.

  10. How do needs and wants differ in terms of how they change over time?

    Needs generally remain constant over time (e.g., the need for food), while wants may change frequently (e.g., wanting tacos one day and sushi the next).

  11. What is a "Product"?

    A product is anything that can be offered to a market to satisfy a need or want. This includes physical goods, services, experiences, events, persons, places, etc.

  12. What defines a "Good"?

    A good is a tangible item that is created, manufactured, and sold to fulfill a need or want.

  13. What defines a "Service"?

    A service is an intangible activity or benefit offered to fulfill a need, usually produced and consumed at the same time.

  14. How does ownership differ between goods and services?

    Goods can be owned and transferred. Services cannot be owned; the customer only owns the experience or result.

  15. How does storage differ between goods and services?

    Goods can be stored for future use. Services are perishable and cannot be stored.

  16. Explain the equation for "Customer Value."

    Customer Value = Perceived Benefits - Perceived Costs. It's the customer's assessment of the product's overall capacity to satisfy their needs.

  17. What are "Perceived Benefits"? Give two examples.

    The bundle of advantages a customer thinks they will get from a product. Examples: product quality, features, brand reputation, customer service.

  18. What are "Perceived Costs"? Give two examples.

    The total sacrifices a customer thinks they have to make to get the product. Examples: monetary price, time spent, effort/energy used.

  19. Define "Satisfaction."

    Satisfaction is a person's feelings of pleasure or disappointment that result from comparing a product's perceived performance to their expectations.

  20. What leads to a customer being "dissatisfied"?

    When the product or service performance is below the customer's expectations.

  21. What leads to a customer being "satisfied"?

    When the product or service performance meets the customer's expectations.

  22. What leads to a customer being "delighted"?

    When the product or service performance exceeds the customer's expectations.

  23. What is an "Exchange" in marketing?

    An exchange is the act of obtaining a desired product from someone by offering something of value in return.

  24. Define what a "Market" is.

    A market is a platform or any arrangement where buyers and sellers interact to exchange goods or services. It consists of all potential customers sharing a particular need or want who are willing and able to engage in exchange.

  25. What is a "Marketplace" versus a "Marketspace"?

    A "Marketplace" is a physical location (like a store). A "Marketspace" is a digital location (like an e-commerce website).

Section 3: Marketing Mix and Philosophies (Questions 41-75)

  1. What is the Marketing Mix?

    The Marketing Mix is the set of controllable, tactical marketing tools that a firm blends to produce the response it wants in the target market.

  2. List the 4 Ps of the traditional Marketing Mix.

    Product, Price, Place, Promotion.

  3. Under "Product," what are three examples of decisions a marketer makes?

    Product variety, quality, design, features, brand name, packaging.

  4. Under "Price," what are three examples of decisions a marketer makes?

    List price, discounts, allowances, payment period, credit terms.

  5. Under "Place," what are three examples of decisions a marketer makes?

    Channels, coverage, locations, inventory, transport.

  6. Under "Promotion," what are three examples of decisions a marketer makes?

    Sales promotion, advertising, public relations, direct marketing.

  7. What are the four components of the "Modern Marketing Management" framework?

    People, Processes, Programs, Performance.

  8. In modern marketing, what does "People" refer to?

    It refers to both the employees who deliver the service/product and the customers themselves, reflecting the importance of internal marketing and customer relationships.

  9. What does "Performance" refer to in the modern framework?

    It refers to the need to measure the outcomes of marketing activities, including both financial metrics (like ROI) and non-financial ones (like brand equity, customer satisfaction).

  10. What is the core belief of the "Production Concept"?

    The core belief is that consumers will favor products that are both affordable and easily accessible. The focus is on high production efficiency and mass distribution.

  11. In which market condition is the Production Concept most viable?

    It is most viable in markets where demand outpaces supply, or where the product cost is too high and needs to be brought down through improved productivity.

  12. What is the core belief of the "Product Concept"?

    The core belief is that consumers will favor products that offer the highest quality, performance, or innovative features.

  13. What is the potential danger of focusing only on the Product Concept?

    The danger is "marketing myopia," where a company becomes so focused on its product that it fails to see changing market needs or new, better ways of satisfying the same core need.

  14. What is the core idea of the "Selling Concept"?

    The core idea is that consumers will not buy enough of a company's products unless they are persuaded through aggressive sales and promotional efforts.

  15. What is the primary focus of companies using the Selling Concept?

    Their focus is on selling what they produce, rather than producing what the market wants. It's an inside-out perspective.

  16. Describe the starting point of the Selling Concept.

    The starting point is the Factory.

  17. Describe the focus of the Selling Concept.

    The focus is on Existing Products.

  18. Describe the means used in the Selling Concept.

    The means are Selling and Promoting.

  19. Describe the end goal of the Selling Concept.

    The end goal is Profit through Sales Volumes.

  20. What is the core idea of the "Marketing Concept"?

    The core idea is that achieving organizational goals depends on understanding the needs and desires of target markets and delivering the desired satisfactions more effectively and efficiently than competitors.

  21. Describe the starting point of the Marketing Concept.

    The starting point is the Market.

  22. Describe the focus of the Marketing Concept.

    The focus is on Customer Needs.

  23. Describe the means used in the Marketing Concept.

    The means is Integrated Marketing.

  24. Describe the end goal of the Marketing Concept.

    The end goal is Profits through Customer Satisfaction.

  25. What is the core idea of the "Societal Marketing Concept"?

    The core idea is that a company's marketing decisions should consider consumers' wants, the company's requirements, and both consumers' and society's long-run interests.

  26. What three factors does the Societal Marketing Concept seek to balance?

    It seeks to balance Company (profits), Consumers (want satisfaction), and Society (human welfare).

  27. Why does the Societal Marketing Concept advocate for social responsibility?

    Because it recognizes that purely satisfying immediate consumer wants may not be good for consumers or society in the long run (e.g., fast food contributing to obesity).

  28. Give an example of a company decision that would align with the Societal Marketing Concept.

    A clothing company switching to organic cotton and fair-trade labor, even if it slightly increases costs, to ensure environmental sustainability and ethical practices.

  29. How does Maslow's hierarchy of needs relate to marketing?

    Marketers use it to understand consumer motivation. Products can be positioned to satisfy different levels of needs. A security system satisfies Safety needs, while a luxury watch might satisfy Esteem needs.

  30. Name the five levels of Maslow's hierarchy from bottom to top.

    1. Physiological, 2. Safety, 3. Love and Belonging, 4. Esteem, 5. Self-actualization.

  31. What does 'homeostasis' refer to under Physiological needs?

    It refers to the body's ability to maintain a stable internal environment (e.g., regulating temperature), which can be supported by products like heating or air conditioning.

  32. How could a social media platform fulfill the 'Love and Belonging' need?

    By facilitating friendships, family connections, and a sense of community in social groups.

  33. What is the ultimate goal of 'Self-actualization'?

    The desire to accomplish everything that one can, to become the most that one can be.

  34. In what ways are 'Value' and 'Satisfaction' related but different?

    Value is a pre-purchase assessment of benefits vs. costs. Satisfaction is a post-purchase assessment of performance vs. expectations. A customer might perceive high value but end up dissatisfied if the product underperforms.

  35. List 10 different types of marketing entities (offerings).

    Goods, Services, Events, Experiences, Persons, Places, Properties, Organizations, Information, and Ideas.

  36. Provide an example of marketing a 'Person'.

    A political candidate running a campaign, or a celebrity like Mahela Jayawardhane building a personal brand for endorsements.

  37. Provide an example of marketing an 'Idea'.

    Public health campaigns promoting the idea of quitting smoking or getting vaccinated.

  38. What is the relationship between 'exchange' and 'relationships' in marketing?

    Modern marketing aims to move beyond simple, one-time exchanges (transactions) to building long-term, profitable relationships with customers.

  39. Why might a company choose to follow a Selling Concept approach for unsought goods like insurance?

    Because consumers do not normally think of buying these goods, a large-scale, aggressive selling and promotion effort is necessary to create awareness and persuade them to purchase.

  40. Explain how the Internet has made the Marketing Concept even more relevant today.

    The internet gives customers more power and information. They can easily research products, compare competitors, and share reviews. This forces companies to be more customer-focused to succeed.

  41. Could a single company use different marketing philosophies for different products? Give a hypothetical example.

    Yes. A pharmaceutical company might use the Product Concept for a groundbreaking new drug (focus on R&D and quality) but use the Selling Concept for an older, over-the-counter medicine facing heavy competition.

  42. What is the difference between Production Timing for Goods vs. Services?

    Goods are typically produced before they are consumed. Services are usually produced and consumed simultaneously (e.g., a haircut).

  43. Why is Quality Control generally harder for services than for goods?

    Because services are often delivered by people, their quality can vary depending on the provider, the customer, and the situation. It is harder to standardize a service than a mass-produced physical good.

  44. What does "Integrated Marketing" mean in the context of the Marketing Concept?

    It means that all the different marketing functions (the 4 Ps) must work together in a coordinated way to serve the customer's needs and deliver a consistent brand message.

  45. How does the subtitle "What It is and Why It Matters to You" frame the presentation's purpose?

    It frames the purpose as being both educational ("What It is") and personally relevant ("Why It Matters to You"), suggesting that marketing is not just a business function but something that affects everyone as a consumer.

  46. Is the "Marketing is ?" slide an effective way to begin defining a concept? Why or why not?

    Yes, it can be effective. It engages the audience by posing a question, encouraging them to think about their own preconceptions before the formal definitions are provided.

  47. Why is "brand name" listed as a component of the Product in the Marketing Mix diagram?

    Because a brand name is a core part of the product's identity and contributes to its perceived value, quality, and differentiation from competitors.

  48. Explain the role of 'financial security' under Safety needs.

    It refers to the need for stability and predictability in one's financial life. This can be addressed by products like insurance, retirement plans, and banking services.

  49. What is the key takeaway from the 'Selling vs. Marketing Concept' diagram?

    The key takeaway is the fundamental shift in perspective: Selling is an 'inside-out' approach (focus on company's products), while Marketing is an 'outside-in' approach (focus on customer's needs).
