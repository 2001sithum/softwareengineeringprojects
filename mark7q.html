Section 1: Product Fundamentals & Levels (Questions 1-25)

  1. What is the first definition of a "product" provided in the presentation?

    A product is any item that satisfies a human need or want and is offered in the marketplace for acquisition, use, or consumption.

  2. What is the second, simpler definition of a product?

    A product is something that can be seen or experienced, created to meet the needs or desires of consumers.

  3. List the 10 forms a product can take.

    Goods, Services, Events, People, Places, Organizations, Ideas, Experiences, Information, and Properties.

  4. What is the "Core Benefit" in the five product levels?

    The fundamental need or benefit the customer is actually buying. It's the core problem-solving service.

  5. What is the "Basic Product" level?

    This is the tangible, physical product or the basic version that includes only the essential attributes to deliver the core benefit.

  6. What is the "Expected Product" level?

    This level includes a set of attributes and conditions that buyers normally expect when they purchase the product.

  7. What is the "Augmented Product" level?

    This level includes additional services and benefits that differentiate the product from competitors. It exceeds customer expectations.

  8. What is the "Potential Product" level?

    This level encompasses all the possible augmentations and transformations the product might undergo in the future.

  9. Using a smartphone, provide an example for the "Core Benefit."

    Communication and connectivity on the go.

  10. Using a smartphone, provide an example for the "Basic Product."

    A device with call, SMS, and internet capabilities.

  11. Using a smartphone, provide an example for the "Expected Product."

    A touchscreen, high-quality camera, apps, decent battery life, and a stable operating system (like iOS).

  12. Using a smartphone, provide an example for the "Augmented Product."

    iCloud storage, Face ID, integration with an ecosystem (MacBook, iPad), AppleCare warranty, and regular software updates.

  13. Using a smartphone, provide an example for the "Potential Product."

    Future features like AI-powered functions, foldable screens, or advanced AR/VR capabilities.

Section 2: Product Classifications (Questions 26-50)

  1. What are the three main classifications of products?

    1. Based on Durability and Tangibility. 2. Consumer Goods Classification. 3. Industrial Goods Classification.

  2. Define "Durable Goods" and give two examples.

    Tangible items that can be used repeatedly and have a long lifespan. Examples: Refrigerators, washing machines, TVs, furniture.

  3. What is a recommended marketing strategy for durable goods?

    Personal selling, providing after-sales services, and emphasizing product quality and guarantees.

  4. Define "Non-durable Goods" and give two examples.

    Items that are consumed quickly and need to be repurchased frequently. Examples: Bread, soap, shampoo.

  5. What is a recommended marketing strategy for non-durable goods?

    Promoting through mass distribution, competitive pricing, and encouraging frequent purchases through advertising and promotions.

  6. Define "Services" and give two examples.

    Intangible items that cannot be touched or stored but fulfill a need or provide a benefit. Examples: Consultancy, electrical repair, legal advice.

  7. What is the primary marketing focus for services?

    Focusing on providing a high-quality service experience to meet consumer expectations, as the service quality is the product itself.

  8. Define "Convenience Goods."

    Frequently purchased, low-cost items that consumers buy with minimal effort and comparison.

  9. Define "Shopping Goods."

    Items for which consumers spend more time comparing quality, price, and style.

  10. Define "Specialty Goods."

    Products with unique characteristics or brand identity, which buyers are willing to make a special effort to purchase.

  11. Define "Unsought Goods."

    Products that consumers do not actively seek or may not think of buying unless prompted through marketing efforts.

  12. What is the typical marketing strategy for unsought goods?

    High promotional and personal selling efforts are required to create demand and awareness.

  13. What are "Materials and Parts" in industrial goods classification?

    Basic inputs for production that include raw materials (e.g., wheat, steel) and manufactured parts (e.g., tires, microchips).

  14. What are "Capital Items"?

    Long-lasting goods that facilitate the production of finished products, categorized into installations (buildings) and equipment (computers, machinery).

  15. What are "Supplies and Services" in industrial goods classification?

    These are items essential for the company's operational activities, such as maintenance supplies (lubricants) and business services (cleaning, legal advice).

Section 3: Product Mix, Packaging & Labeling (Questions 51-75)

  1. Define "Product Mix."

    A product mix is the complete set of all products and items a particular seller offers for sale.

  2. What is "Product Width"?

    This refers to the number of different product lines a company offers. In the Coca-Cola example, the width is two: "Soft Drinks" and "Minute Maid."

  3. What is "Product Length"?

    This refers to the total number of individual items within the product mix. In the example, the length is nine (5 soft drinks + 4 juices).

  4. What is "Product Depth"?

    This refers to the variety of versions offered for each product in a line, such as different sizes, flavors, or formulations. For example, Diet Coke and Coke Zero add depth to the Coca-Cola product.

  5. What is "Packaging"?

    Packaging refers to the overall process of creating and developing a protective cover or container for products.

  6. Describe Primary Packaging.

    This is the immediate container that is in direct contact with the product, providing direct protection (e.g., the toothpaste tube).

  7. Describe Secondary Packaging.

    This is the layer of packaging that contains the primary packaging, providing additional support and branding (e.g., the cardboard box the toothpaste tube comes in).

  8. Describe Tertiary/Bulk Packaging.

    This is packaging used for larger-scale transport and handling, such as a large shipping container or carton holding multiple secondary packages.

  9. What is the "Functionality Check" for packaging?

    This involves evaluating factors like packaging size, material, durability, color, cost, and graphics to ensure it protects the product and satisfies the customer.

  10. What are the "Environmental and Health Considerations" for packaging?

    Packaging should ideally be recyclable, avoid contributing to pollution, and be safe for consumer health (e.g., non-toxic materials).

  11. What does "Convenience for the Customer" mean in packaging?

    Packaging should not create unnecessary inconvenience due to bulkiness, excessive weight, or being difficult to open and use.

  12. What is "Labeling"?

    Labeling is an essential component of packaging that provides necessary details about the product.

  13. What are the three primary functions of labeling?

    1. Product and Brand Identification. 2. Product Categorization. 3. Product Promotion.

  14. Explain the difference between a "warranty" and a "guarantee."

    A warranty is a formal, often written, promise to repair or replace a faulty product within a specific time frame. A guarantee is a broader promise of satisfaction that may include a full refund if the customer is not happy, covering more than just defects.

Section 4: Branding, NPD, and PLC (Questions 76-101)

  1. Define a "brand."

    "A brand is a name, term, design, symbol, or any other feature that identifies one seller's goods or services as distinct from those of other sellers."

  2. What are the four main elements a brand comprises?

    Brand Name, Brand Symbol, Trademark, and Ownership.

  3. List five benefits of branding for a business.

    1. Ability to differentiate products. 2. Providing a legally protected identity. 3. Adding unique attributes. 4. Helping with market segmentation. 5. Assisting in promotional activities.

  4. What are three important considerations for selecting a brand name?

    1. Easy to remember. 2. Must have a meaningful interpretation. 3. Should be legally protectable.

  5. What is the New Product Development (NPD) process?

    It is a systematic process that takes a new product idea from conception to launch. It acts as a funnel to screen out unviable ideas and refine promising ones.

  6. List the eight steps of the NPD process.

    1. New Product Strategy, 2. Idea Generation, 3. Idea Screening, 4. Business Analysis, 5. Development, 6. Test Marketing, 7. Commercialisation, 8. New Product (Launch).

  7. What is the purpose of "Idea Screening"?

    To filter out poor or unfeasible ideas quickly to avoid wasting resources on them.

  8. What happens during "Test Marketing"?

    The product and its proposed marketing program are introduced into realistic market settings to gauge consumer response before a full-scale launch.

  9. What is the Product Life Cycle (PLC)?

    The Product Life Cycle describes the stages a product goes through from its introduction into the market until it is eventually removed.

  10. What are the five stages of the PLC?

    1. Product Development, 2. Introduction, 3. Growth, 4. Maturity, 5. Decline.

  11. Describe the "Sales" and "Profits" curves during the "Introduction" stage.

    Sales are low and grow slowly. Profits are negative due to high development and marketing costs.

  12. Describe the "Sales" and "Profits" curves during the "Growth" stage.

    Sales rise rapidly. Profits also rise rapidly and typically peak towards the end of this stage as economies of scale are achieved.

  13. Describe the "Sales" and "Profits" curves during the "Maturity" stage.

    Sales peak and then begin to level off or decline slightly. Profits decline due to increased competition and marketing expenditure to defend market share.

  14. Describe the "Sales" and "Profits" curves during the "Decline" stage.

    Both sales and profits decline significantly.
