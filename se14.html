Software Cost Estimation

Predicting the Resources Required for Development

Fundamentals of Costing & Pricing

Software Cost Components

In project planning, a project is split into activities. For each activity, we must estimate the required Effort, Time, and Cost. The primary factor is effort cost.

  • Hardware and software costs: Licenses, cloud services, development tools.
  • Travel and training costs.
  • Effort costs (Dominant Factor):
    • Salaries of engineers.
    • Overheads: Building costs (heating, lighting), networking, shared facilities (library, staff restaurant), social security, employee benefits, and support staff.

Rule of Thumb:

Total Project Costs ≈ 2 × Software Engineers' Salaries

Costing vs. Pricing

There is no simple relationship between the cost to develop software and the price charged to the customer. Software Price ≠ Cost + Profit.

Pricing is influenced by broader organizational, economic, political, and business considerations.

Pricing FactorDescription
Cost estimate uncertaintyIf an organization is unsure of its cost estimate, it may add a contingency to the price.
Contractual termsIf the developer retains ownership of the source code, the price may be lower.
Requirements volatilityIf requirements are likely to change, a lower initial price may be quoted to win the contract, with higher prices charged for later changes.
Financial healthA developer in financial difficulty may lower their price just to win a contract and stay in business.
Market opportunityA low price may be quoted to enter a new market segment, with the hope of making more profit on future projects.

Software Productivity

Measuring Development Rate

Productivity is a measure of the rate at which engineers produce software and associated documentation. The goal is to measure useful functionality produced per unit of time (e.g., person-month).

Productivity Measures:

  • Size-related measures: Based on the output of the process, such as Lines of Code (LOC).
  • Function-related measures: Based on an estimate of the software's functionality. Function Points (FPs) are the best-known measure of this type.

Problems with Lines of Code (LOC)

  • Ambiguity: What counts as a "line of code" in modern languages?
  • Language Dependence: The same functionality takes more lines in a low-level language than in a high-level one.
  • Verbosity Bias: It suggests that programmers who write verbose code are more productive than those who write compact, efficient code.

Problems with Function Points (FPs)

  • Subjectivity: The calculation depends on estimating factors like external inputs/outputs, user interactions, and interfaces. This depends heavily on the estimator.
  • Complexity: The raw count is modified by a project complexity factor.
  • Automation is Impossible: It cannot be counted automatically from the code.

Estimation Techniques

Approaches to Estimation

There is no simple way to make an accurate estimate, especially early in a project when information is inadequate, the technology is new, or the team is unknown. It's best to use several methods and compare the results.

TechniqueDescription
Algorithmic cost modellingUses a mathematical formula (based on historical data) that relates a software metric (usually size) to project cost.
Expert judgementOne or more experts use their experience to predict costs. The process iterates until a consensus is reached. Pros: Cheap and can be accurate. Cons: Very inaccurate if no true experts are available.
Estimation by analogyThe cost is computed by comparing the project to similar completed projects. Pros: Can be very accurate. Cons: Requires a well-maintained database of historical project data.
Parkinson's LawThe project costs whatever resources are available ("work expands to fill the time available"). Pros: No overspending. Cons: The system is usually unfinished.
Pricing to winThe cost is estimated to be whatever the customer has to spend. Pros: You get the contract. Cons: The customer is unlikely to get the system they really want.

Top-down Estimation

Starts at the system level, assessing overall functionality. It takes into account system-level costs like integration and documentation but can underestimate difficult low-level technical problems.

Bottom-up Estimation

Starts at the component level, estimating the effort for each part and summing them up. This can be accurate if the system has been designed in detail but may underestimate system-level costs.

Algorithmic Cost Modelling

Using Formulas to Estimate Cost

This technique uses a mathematical function to estimate cost based on product, project, and process attributes. The most common input attribute is software size (e.g., LOC or Function Points).

General Formula Structure:

Effort = A × SizeB × M

  • A: A constant factor derived from historical project data.
  • Size: An assessment of the product size (e.g., in KLOC - Kilo Lines of Code).
  • B: A value that reflects the disproportionate effort for larger projects (usually between 1 and 1.5).
  • M: A multiplier that reflects the characteristics of the product, project, and process (e.g., complexity, staff experience, tools used).

This formulaic approach forms the basis of many cost estimation models, including the COCOMO (Constructive Cost Model) models, which are among the most well-known and documented algorithmic models.
