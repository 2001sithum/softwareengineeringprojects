What is Consumer Behavior?

Consumer behavior refers to the actions and decisions that people make when they are purchasing or using products.

More specifically, consumer behavior is the actions and decisions that people or households make when they choose, buy, use, and dispose of a product or service.

Alternative Definitions

The decision process and physical activities individuals engage in when evaluating, acquiring, using, or disposing of goods and services. Loudon & Bitta (1993)
The dynamic interaction of affect and cognition, behavior, and environmental events by which human beings conduct the exchange aspect of their lives. Peter (1996)
CB is the study of individuals, groups or organizations and processes they use to select, secure, use and dispose of products, services, experiences or ideas to satisfy needs and impacts that these processes have on the consumer and the society. Hawkins (2005)

Consumer Behavior as a Process

Consumer behavior can be viewed as a three-stage process, with different issues and questions arising from both the consumer's and the marketer's perspective at each stage.

STAGE CONSUMER'S PERSPECTIVE MARKETER'S PERSPECTIVE
PRE-PURCHASE ISSUES How does a consumer decide about needing a product? How are consumer attitudes formed/changed?
PURCHASE ISSUES Is product acquisition a stressful or pleasant experience? How do situational factors affect purchase decision?
POST-PURCHASE ISSUES Does the product provide pleasure or perform its function? How is the product disposed of? What determines customer satisfaction and repurchase?

Consumer Roles and Types

Roles: Buyers, Payers, and Users

The marketplace activities of individuals entail three distinct functions:

1. The Consumer (User)
Who consumes or uses the product.
2. The Buyer
Who undertakes activities to procure or obtain the product.
3. The Payer
Who provides the money (or other value) to obtain the product.

Major Types of Consumers

  • Personal Consumers: Individuals who purchase products for their own use or for their household (end-users).
  • Organizational Consumers: Entities that buy products to help run their operations. This includes:
    • Business organizations (for-profit)
    • Non-profit organizations
    • Government agencies
    • Institutional buyers (e.g., schools, hospitals)

The Wheel of Consumer Analysis

A successful marketing strategy depends on understanding the dynamic interplay between what consumers think and feel, what they do, and what is happening in the world around them.

The Three Core Elements

  • Affect and Cognition: Refers to the internal, psychological responses consumers have, including feelings (e.g., love, anger) and thinking (e.g., beliefs, knowledge).
  • Behavior: Refers to the overt acts or actions of consumers, such as buying, visiting a store, or watching commercials.
  • Environment: Refers to the complex physical and social stimuli in the external world, including things, places, and people that influence consumers.

The Consumer Buying Process

The consumer buying process is a five-stage model that illustrates the steps a consumer typically takes when making a purchase decision.

1. Problem Recognition

This is the first step where a person notices they need something or have a problem to solve. It could be because they ran out of a product, want a better version, or have a specific issue to fix.

2. Information Search

After realizing they need something, consumers begin looking for information. They may search online, ask friends and family for advice, read product reviews, or pay attention to ads.

3. Evaluation of Alternatives

At this stage, consumers look at different options or brands that can meet their needs. They compare the advantages and disadvantages of each and usually narrow down their choices.

4. Purchase Decision

After comparing their options, consumers decide what to buy. Their choice can be influenced by things like product availability, price, discounts, and personal preferences.

5. Post-Purchase Evaluation & Behavior

Consumers think about whether they are happy with what they bought. They check if the product met their expectations. If satisfied, they may buy again; if not, they might return it or leave a negative review. This also includes sharing experiences through reviews or referrals.

A Simple Model of Consumer Decision Making

This model integrates the buying process with external influences (like marketing and culture) and internal psychological factors to provide a comprehensive view of how consumers make decisions.
