1. Introduction to Product

A product is any item that satisfies a human need or want and is offered in the marketplace for acquisition, use, or consumption. It is something that can be seen or experienced, created to meet the needs or desires of consumers.

Products can take various forms:

Goods (tangible items)
Services (intangible activities)
Events (concerts, festivals)
People (celebrities, experts)
Places (tourist destinations)
Organizations (non-profits, clubs)
Ideas (concepts, philosophies)
Information (data, news)
Experiences (theme parks, travel)
Properties (real estate, stocks)

2. The Five Product Levels

Marketers plan their product offerings at five levels. Each level adds more customer value, and the five constitute a customer-value hierarchy. Let's use an iPhone as an example.

  • 1. Core Benefit: The fundamental need or benefit the customer is buying. Communication and connectivity on the go.
  • 2. Basic Product: The basic version of the product that includes only the essential attributes. A smartphone with call, SMS, and internet capabilities.
  • 3. Expected Product: The set of attributes and conditions buyers normally expect. Touchscreen, high-quality camera, apps, decent battery life, iOS system.
  • 4. Augmented Product: Additional services and benefits that differentiate the product. iCloud storage, Face ID, Apple ecosystem, AppleCare warranty, regular software updates.
  • 5. Potential Product: All the possible augmentations and transformations the product might undergo in the future. Future features like AI-powered functions, foldable screens, advanced AR/VR capabilities.

3. Product Classification

Products are classified based on factors such as durability, tangibility, and usage. This is essential for creating effective marketing strategies.

i) Based on Durability and Tangibility

Durable Goods

Tangible items used repeatedly over a long lifespan (e.g., refrigerators, TVs, furniture). Strategy focuses on personal selling and quality assurance.

Non-durable Goods

Items consumed quickly and repurchased frequently (e.g., soap, bread, shampoo). Strategy focuses on mass distribution and frequent reminders.

Services

Intangible items that fulfill a need (e.g., consultancy, electrical repair). Strategy focuses on service quality and customer experience.

ii) Consumer Goods Classification

Convenience Goods

Frequently purchased, low-cost items bought with minimal effort (e.g., soft drinks, newspapers).

Shopping Goods

Items for which consumers spend time comparing quality, price, and style (e.g., furniture, clothing).

Specialty Goods

Products with unique characteristics or brand identity that buyers make a special effort to purchase (e.g., luxury cars, high-end electronics).

Unsought Goods

Products consumers don't actively seek or think of buying unless prompted (e.g., life insurance, smoke detectors).

iii) Industrial Goods Classification

Materials and Parts

Basic inputs for production, including raw materials (wheat, steel) and manufactured parts (tires).

Capital Items

Long-lasting goods that facilitate production, like installations (buildings) and equipment (computers, machinery).

Supplies and Services

Items essential for operational activities, such as maintenance supplies and business services.

4. Product Mix

The product mix is the complete set of all products and items a particular seller offers for sale. It can be described in terms of its width, length, and depth.

  • Product Width: The number of different product lines a company offers. In the diagram, the width is two: "Soft Drinks" and "Minute Maid."
  • Product Length: The total number of individual products across all lines. Here, the length is 9 (5 soft drinks + 4 juices).
  • Product Depth: The number of variations within each product line (e.g., sizes, flavors). Diet Coke and Coke Zero add depth to the Coca-Cola product.

5. Packaging, Labeling, and Warranties

Packaging

Packaging refers to the overall process of creating and developing a protective cover or container for products. It has several levels and key objectives.

  • Primary Packaging: The immediate container of the product (e.g., the sealed bag holding milk powder).
  • Secondary Packaging: The material that protects the primary packaging (e.g., the cardboard box holding the bag).
  • Tertiary/Bulk Packaging: Used for shipping and handling (e.g., a large shipping container holding multiple boxes).

Labeling

Labeling is an essential component of packaging that provides necessary details about the product. It may include the brand name, size, instructions, and nutritional information.

Product and Brand Identification

Helping to identify the product and its brand.

Product Categorization

Classifying the product for easier recognition.

Product Promotion

Attractively designed labels can aid in product promotion.

Warranty vs. Guarantee

While often used interchangeably, these terms have different meanings. A warranty is a written promise to repair or replace a product if it fails within a specific period. A guarantee is a broader promise of satisfaction, often implying a full refund if the customer is unhappy.

6. Branding

"A brand is a name, term, design, symbol, or any other feature that identifies one seller's goods or services as distinct from those of other sellers."

Benefits of Branding for Businesses:

  • Ability to differentiate products.
  • Providing a legally protected identity (trademark).
  • Adding unique attributes to the business.
  • Helping with market segmentation.
  • Assisting in promotional activities.
  • Enhancing business reputation.

7. New Product Development (NPD)

The New Product Development process is a funnel where many ideas are screened and refined to produce one successful new product.

8. Product Life Cycle (PLC)

The Product Life Cycle describes the stages a product goes through from when it is first introduced into the market until it is removed. The stages are Development, Introduction, Growth, Maturity, and Decline.
