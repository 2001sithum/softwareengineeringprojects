The STP Process: The Foundation of Target Marketing

Target marketing is a strategic process that moves away from a "one-size-fits-all" approach (Mass Marketing) to a more focused and efficient model. This is achieved through the three core steps of the STP process.

Mass Marketing vs. Segmented (Target) Marketing

Aspect Mass Marketing Segmented Marketing
Audience Broad, undefined range of consumers One or two well-defined consumer groups
Goal Build broad Brand Image Achieve specific Product Positioning
Targeting Focuses on common needs Benefits tailored to a specific target audience
Tracking Cannot easily track conversions Can track conversions (especially with digital tools)
Duration Long-term consistency Shorter-term, targeted activations

1. Market Segmentation: Dividing the Market

Segmentation is the process of dividing a broad market into smaller, well-defined groups (segments) with shared characteristics. This allows marketers to understand their audience better and tailor their efforts more effectively.

Demographic Segmentation

This method groups customers based on objective, statistical data.

  • Variables: Age, income, gender, education level, family size, occupation.
  • Use Case: A luxury car brand might target high-income individuals aged 40-60.

Geographic Segmentation

This method divides the market based on geographical location.

  • Variables: Country, region, city, climate, urban/rural.
  • Use Case: A company selling snow jackets would target colder regions, while a surfboard company would focus on coastal areas.

Psychographic Segmentation

This method groups customers based on their intrinsic traits and psychological characteristics.

  • Variables: Interests, values, lifestyles, personality traits, opinions.
  • Use Case: An adventure travel company might target thrill-seekers who value experiences over possessions.

Behavioral Segmentation

This method groups customers based on their purchasing behavior, usage patterns, and brand loyalty.

  • Variables: Purchasing habits, brand loyalty, usage rate (heavy vs. light user), occasion.
  • Use Case: A coffee shop could offer a loyalty card to reward frequent buyers and send special offers to occasional visitors.

Needs-Based & Benefits Segmentation

This method groups customers based on the underlying needs they are trying to fulfill or the specific benefits they seek from a product.

  • Needs: Functional (solving a problem) vs. Psychological (emotional satisfaction).
  • Benefits: Economic (saving money), Functional (performance), Emotional (self-image).
  • Use Case: A gym could segment its audience based on needs: weight loss, muscle building, or social interaction.

2. Market Targeting: Selecting the Segment(s)

Targeting is the process of evaluating the various market segments and choosing the most promising ones to focus your marketing efforts on. It's about deciding where you can win.

Selection Criteria

  • Size: Is the segment large enough to be profitable after considering marketing and production costs?
  • Accessibility: Can you effectively reach the segment through your chosen marketing channels?
  • Measurability: Is it possible to track your marketing performance within the segment?
  • Profitability: Does the segment have sufficient purchasing power and willingness to buy?
Example: Athletic Shoe Company
After segmenting the market, an athletic shoe company decides to target two key segments:
1. Young Athletes (15-25): This segment prioritizes style, brand reputation, and affordability.
2. Serious Runners (25-45): This segment values performance, innovation, and comfort, and is willing to pay a premium.

3. Market Positioning: Creating a Clear Identity

Positioning refers to the process of creating a distinct and desirable image and identity for a product or service in the minds of the target customers. It's about how you want your audience to perceive your brand relative to competitors.

Key Elements of Positioning

  • Target Market: Who you are trying to reach.
  • Points of Difference (POD): Unique features, benefits, or attributes that set your offering apart from competitors. These are your unique selling propositions.
  • Points of Parity (POP): Essential features or benefits that a brand must offer to even be considered a viable option in a market. These are the "table stakes."
  • Value Proposition: A clear statement that defines the value your product delivers and how it solves customer problems.
Example: Volvo's Positioning
Volvo has successfully positioned itself as the leader in automotive safety. While it has points of parity with other car brands (e.g., has four wheels, an engine), its primary point of difference and core value proposition is "Your safety is a top priority."

Positioning Map

A positioning map is a visual tool used to understand the competitive landscape and identify potential gaps in the market based on key dimensions that customers value.
