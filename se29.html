Section 1: Fundamentals of Software Project Management

  1. 1. What is the primary concern of software project management?

    Ensuring that software is delivered on time, on schedule, and in accordance with the requirements of the organization.

  2. 2. Why is project management particularly needed for software development?

    Because software development is always subject to budget and schedule constraints set by the developing organization.

  3. 3. List three key 'distinctions' or challenges that make software management different from other types of engineering management.

    1. The product is intangible. 2. The product is uniquely flexible. 3. The development process is not standardized.

  4. 4. Explain what it means for the software product to be 'intangible'.

    Unlike a physical product like a bridge, you cannot see or touch the software product itself. Progress is difficult to visualize and measure without formal mechanisms.

  5. 5. Explain what it means for the software product to be 'uniquely flexible'.

    It is usually possible to change software at any stage of its development, which means requirements are often not stable, making planning difficult.

  6. 6. What does it mean that many software projects are 'one-off' projects?

    Each project is often new and different from previous projects, so it is hard to reuse experience and established processes.

  7. 7. Despite its distinctions, what 'commonalities' does software management share with other engineering disciplines?

    Many general project management techniques are equally applicable, and technically complex systems in any field tend to suffer from similar problems (e.g., complexity, changing requirements).

  8. 8. Who sets the budget and schedule constraints for a software project?

    The organization developing the software.

Section 2: Management Activities

  1. 9. List six fundamental management activities.

    1. Proposal writing. 2. Project planning and scheduling. 3. Project costing. 4. Project monitoring and reviews. 5. Personnel selection and evaluation. 6. Report writing and presentations.

  2. 10. What is the purpose of 'proposal writing'?

    To describe the objectives of a project and how it will be carried out, often as part of a bid to win a contract from an external customer.

  3. 11. What is 'project costing'?

    The activity of predicting the resources (time, effort, materials) required to carry out the project.

  4. 12. What does 'project monitoring and reviews' involve?

    Continuously checking the progress of the project against its plan and reviewing the quality and standards of the software being developed.

  5. 13. What is the main responsibility of a project manager regarding report writing?

    To report on the project's progress to both the client and their own contractor organization.

  6. 14. What skills must a manager have for presentations?

    They must be able to present critical project information clearly and concisely during progress reviews.

  7. 15. What is 'personnel selection and evaluation'?

    The activity of choosing the right people for the project team and evaluating their performance.

  8. 16. List three reasons why it might not be possible to appoint the 'ideal' people to a project.

    1. The project budget may not allow for highly-paid staff. 2. Staff with the appropriate experience may not be available. 3. The organization may wish to use the project to develop the skills of junior employees.

  9. 17. What is the most time-consuming project management activity?

    Project planning.

Section 3: Project Planning

  1. 18. Why is project planning a continuous activity?

    Because plans must be regularly revised as new information becomes available and as the project progresses. It is not a one-time, upfront activity.

  2. 19. What is the purpose of the main software project plan?

    It is concerned with the overall schedule and budget of the project.

  3. 20. List three types of supporting project plans.

    Quality plan, Validation plan, Configuration management plan, Maintenance plan, Staff development plan.

  4. 21. What are the first three steps in the project planning process?

    1. Establish the project constraints. 2. Make initial assessments of the project parameters. 3. Define project milestones and deliverables.

  5. 22. What happens in the main loop of the project planning process?

    The manager draws up a schedule, initiates activities, reviews progress, revises estimates, and updates the schedule. This loop continues until the project is complete.

  6. 23. What three things does the main project plan set out?

    1. The resources available to the project. 2. The work breakdown. 3. A schedule for the work.

  7. 24. List four key sections of a project plan structure.

    Introduction, Project organisation, Risk analysis, Work breakdown, Project schedule, Monitoring and reporting mechanisms.

  8. 25. What is a 'milestone'?

    The end-point of a process activity. It is a predictable state where a formal report of progress can be presented to management.

  9. 26. What is a 'deliverable'?

    A project result that is delivered to customers (e.g., a requirements document, an executable system).

  10. 27. Give an example of a milestone in the requirements engineering process.

    The completion of the 'Feasibility report' or the 'Architectural design' document.

Section 4: Project Scheduling

  1. 28. What is the first step in project scheduling?

    To split the project into tasks and estimate the time and resources required to complete each task.

  2. 29. Why is it important to organize tasks concurrently?

    To make optimal use of the workforce, allowing multiple team members to work in parallel.

  3. 30. Why should task dependencies be minimized?

    To avoid delays caused by one task having to wait for another to complete.

  4. 31. What does project scheduling often depend on?

    The project manager's intuition and experience.

  5. 32. List the steps in the project scheduling process diagram.

    Identify activities -> Identify dependencies -> Estimate resources -> Allocate people -> Create charts.

  6. 33. Why is estimating the cost of developing a solution hard?

    Because it is hard to estimate the difficulty of the problems that will be encountered during development.

  7. 34. What is the relationship between productivity and the number of people on a task?

    Productivity is not proportional to the number of people working on a task. Doubling the team size does not halve the time.

  8. 35. What is Brooks's Law?

    Adding people to a late project makes it later.

  9. 36. Why does adding people to a late project make it later?

    Because of the communication overheads required to bring the new staff up to speed and integrate them into the team.

  10. 37. What should a manager always allow for in planning?

    Contingency, because the unexpected always happens.

  11. 38. What are bar charts (Gantt charts) used for?

    To show the project schedule against calendar time.

  12. 39. What do activity networks (PERT charts) show?

    They show the dependencies between tasks and the critical path.

  13. 40. What is the 'critical path'?

    The sequence of tasks which determines the minimum time to complete the project. Any delay in a critical path task will delay the entire project.

  14. 41. In the example activity timeline (Gantt chart), which task is on the critical path and determines the finish date?

    Task T8 is the longest task on the critical path, and its completion date of 19/9/03 determines the project's finish date.

Section 5: Risk Management

  1. 42. What is 'risk management'?

    The process of identifying risks and drawing up plans to minimize their effect on a project.

  2. 43. What is a 'risk'?

    A probability that some adverse circumstance will occur.

  3. 44. List the three categories of risks.

    1. Project risks. 2. Product risks. 3. Business risks.

  4. 45. What do 'project risks' affect?

    The project schedule or resources. Example: Staff turnover, hardware unavailability.

  5. 46. What do 'product risks' affect?

    The quality or performance of the software being developed. Example: Requirements change, size underestimate.

  6. 47. What do 'business risks' affect?

    The organization developing or procuring the software. Example: Technology change, a competitor releasing a product first.

  7. 48. List the four stages of the risk management process.

    1. Risk identification. 2. Risk analysis. 3. Risk planning. 4. Risk monitoring.

  8. 49. What is 'risk identification'?

    Identifying potential project, product, and business risks.

  9. 50. What is 'risk analysis'?

    Assessing the likelihood (probability) and consequences (seriousness) of each risk.

  10. 51. What is 'risk planning'?

    Drawing up plans and strategies to avoid or minimize the effects of the risk.

  11. 52. What is 'risk monitoring'?

    Regularly assessing the identified risks throughout the project to see if their probability or impact has changed.

  12. 53. List three types of risks identified during risk identification.

    Technology risks, People risks, Organisational risks, Requirements risks, Estimation risks.

  13. 54. Give an example of a 'People risk'.

    Key staff are ill and unavailable at critical times; it is impossible to recruit staff with the required skills.

  14. 55. Give an example of an 'Estimation risk'.

    The time required to develop the software is underestimated; the size of the software is underestimated.

  15. 56. List the possible levels of risk probability.

    Very low, low, moderate, high, or very high.

  16. 57. List the possible levels of risk effect.

    Catastrophic, serious, tolerable, or insignificant.

  17. 58. What are the three types of strategies developed during risk planning?

    1. Avoidance strategies. 2. Minimisation strategies. 3. Contingency plans.

  18. 59. What is an 'avoidance strategy'?

    A strategy where the probability that the risk will arise is reduced (e.g., using a more reliable technology).

  19. 60. What is a 'minimisation strategy'?

    A strategy where the impact of the risk on the project or product will be reduced if it occurs (e.g., reorganizing the team so there is more knowledge overlap).

  20. 61. What is a 'contingency plan'?

    A plan to deal with a risk *if* it arises. It is a backup plan.

  21. 62. What is a 'risk indicator'?

    A potential indicator or warning sign that a particular type of risk may be materializing.

  22. 63. Give an example of a potential indicator for a 'People risk'.

    Poor staff morale, poor relationships amongst team members, or high job availability in the market (which could lead to staff turnover).

  23. 64. Give an example of a potential indicator for an 'Estimation risk'.

    Failure to meet the agreed schedule or failure to clear reported defects at the expected rate.

  24. 65. What is a key point about the intangible nature of software?

    The intangible nature of software causes significant problems for management in tracking progress and quality.

  25. 66. What are the most significant activities of a software project manager?

    Planning, estimating, and scheduling.

  26. 67. What is a key point about a project milestone?

    It is a predictable state where a formal report of progress is presented to management.

  27. 68. What is a key point about project scheduling?

    It involves preparing various graphical representations (like Gantt and PERT charts) showing project activities, their durations, and staffing.

  28. 69. What is a key point about risk management?

    It is concerned with identifying risks and planning to ensure that these risks do not develop into major threats.

  29. 70. Is good project management important for project success?

    Yes, the slides state that good project management is essential for project success.

  30. 71. What does "contingency" in planning mean?

    Adding extra time or resources to the plan to account for unforeseen problems or risks.

  31. 72. What is a 'work breakdown structure' (WBS)?

    A hierarchical decomposition of the total scope of work to be carried out by the project team. It is the output of splitting the project into tasks.

  32. 73. What is a 'Quality Plan'?

    A supporting plan that describes the quality procedures and standards that will be used in a project.

  33. 74. What is a 'Validation Plan'?

    A supporting plan that describes the approach, resources, and schedule for system validation and testing.

  34. 75. What is a 'Configuration Management Plan'?

    A supporting plan that describes the procedures and standards for configuration management.

  35. 76. What is a 'Maintenance Plan'?

    A supporting plan that predicts the maintenance requirements, costs, and effort needed for the system.

  36. 77. What is a 'Staff Development Plan'?

    A supporting plan that describes how the skills and experience of the project team members will be developed.

  37. 78. In the activity network diagram, what does an arrow between two tasks represent?

    A dependency. The task at the head of the arrow cannot start until the task at the tail of the arrow is complete.

  38. 79. In the activity timeline (Gantt chart), what do the diamonds represent?

    Milestones (e.g., M1, M2, M3).

  39. 80. How does the activity timeline show slack or float time?

    The light blue portion of the bar represents the slack time for a taskâ€”the amount of time it can be delayed without affecting the project finish date. Tasks on the critical path have no slack.

  40. 81. What is a risk management strategy for 'staff illness'?

    Reorganise the team so there is more overlap of work and people understand each other's jobs, reducing dependency on a single person.

  41. 82. What is a risk management strategy for 'requirements changes'?

    Derive traceability information to assess the impact of changes, and use design principles like information hiding to minimize the ripple effect of changes.

  42. 83. What is a risk management strategy for 'underestimated development time'?

    Investigate buying-in pre-made components or using a program generator to accelerate development.

  43. 84. Why is a task size of "a week or two" recommended in scheduling?

    If tasks are too small, the overhead of managing them is too high. If they are too large, it is difficult to judge progress accurately. A week or two is a good balance.

  44. 85. What is the input to the 'Identify activities' step in the scheduling process?

    The software requirements.

  45. 86. What is the output of the 'Create project charts' step?

    Activity charts and bar charts.

  46. 87. In the project planning process loop, what triggers a 'technical review and possible revision'?

    This is triggered 'if problems arise' during the project's execution.

  47. 88. What is a strategy to handle 'defective components' that were planned for reuse?

    Replace the potentially defective components with bought-in components of known reliability.

  48. 89. What is a strategy for dealing with 'recruitment problems'?

    Alert the customer of potential difficulties and delays, and investigate buying-in components to reduce the amount of development work required.

  49. 90. What is a strategy for mitigating the risk of 'organisational restructuring'?

    Prepare a briefing document for senior management showing how the project is making a very important contribution to the goals of the business, to ensure its continued support.

  50. 91. What is a potential indicator for a 'Technology' risk?

    Late delivery of hardware or support software; many reported technology problems.

  51. 92. What is a potential indicator for a 'Tools' risk?

    Reluctance by team members to use tools; complaints about CASE tools; demands for higher-powered workstations.

  52. 93. What is a potential indicator for a 'Requirements' risk?

    Many requirements change requests; customer complaints.

  53. 94. What is the main conflict in project staffing?

    The need for experienced, ideal staff versus constraints like budget limitations and the desire to develop junior employees.

  54. 95. How is progress judged in activity organization?

    Activities should be organized to produce tangible outputs (deliverables and milestones) so that management can judge progress objectively.

  55. 96. What is the key point about the iterative nature of planning?

    Planning and estimating are iterative processes which continue throughout the course of a project.

  56. 97. What is the final step in the risk management process before the cycle repeats?

    Risk monitoring, which provides feedback (a risk assessment) that can lead back to further risk analysis and planning.

  57. 98. What output from risk analysis feeds into risk planning?

    A prioritized risk list.

  58. 99. What is the difference between a task duration and a task dependency?

    Duration is the estimated time a task will take to complete (e.g., 8 days). Dependency is the relationship between tasks, specifying which tasks must be completed before another can begin.

  59. 100. What is the overall message of the "Software Project Management" presentation?

    That successful software development requires a disciplined management approach covering planning, scheduling, and risk management to navigate the unique challenges of building intangible, flexible products under budget and time constraints.
